Is the Algorithm Killing Originality or Freeing the Next Generation?

By Amara Osei · Opinion Editor · 2 Hrs Ago HOT TAKE

We scroll, we like, we share — and somewhere in that endless loop, the ideas that were supposed to be ours quietly become everyone else's. The feed is the new editor-in-chief, and nobody voted for it. Yet a counter-argument is gaining ground among students here: that the same algorithm which flattens taste also surfaces voices that would never have made it into the old gatekept press. Which side are you on?

"The most dangerous opinion is one you were nudged into having without ever noticing the nudge."

Consider the campus newspaper of a decade ago. Three editors decided what mattered. If your voice didn't fit their vision, it didn't exist. Today, a first-year student with a hot take can reach ten thousand peers by midnight. The algorithm doesn't care about your credentials — it cares about engagement. That's terrifying. It's also liberating.

The digital age has transformed how we consume and create content. Photo by J. Martinez.

But here's the uncomfortable truth: we're trading one gatekeeper for another. The algorithm isn't neutral. It's designed to keep you scrolling, not thinking. Every "For You" page is a mirror reflecting your biases back at you, slightly amplified. The question isn't whether algorithms are good or bad — it's whether we're aware enough to fight back.

Some argue that the solution is digital literacy. Teach students to recognize manipulation, to seek out opposing views, to pause before sharing. Others say that's naive — that the system is designed to bypass rational thought entirely. The dopamine hit of a viral post is stronger than any fact-check.

What's clear is that we're in the middle of an experiment. Nobody knows where this ends. But the students who will thrive are those who can hold two thoughts at once: that the algorithm gives us a voice, and that it also shapes what we say.
